# Company as an Accountability System

A company assigns rights, duties, incentives, and consequences. That is what makes a business more than a loose collaboration: someone can decide, someone can be monitored, someone owns the upside, and someone bears the downside.

## Sections

### Core Idea

Jensen and Meckling are the clearest source for this lens because they focus on agency costs and ownership structure. The [OECD Principles of Corporate Governance](https://legalinstruments.oecd.org/public/doc/151/body-text.en.html) adds a practical governance view by describing how objectives are set, performance is monitored, and relationships among management, boards, shareholders, and stakeholders are structured.

### Details

Accountability is the difference between "people are working on things" and "a company is operating." A real company assigns ownership, defines authority, sets reporting expectations, and creates consequences for performance and failure. Without that layer, coordination breaks down and incentives drift.

This is why cap tables, boards, management systems, financial reporting, and performance reviews exist. They are not just administrative overhead. They are mechanisms for making action legible and responsibility assignable.

This lens is essential for the book because agent-heavy organizations will not escape the accountability problem. If anything, they may intensify it. When many agents can act quickly, the human principal needs even clearer review loops, escalation rules, and ownership boundaries.

This lens matters for the book because agent-native companies will still need accountability. Even if more work is delegated to software agents, the human layer still needs to decide who has authority, who reviews output, and who is responsible when the system fails.
