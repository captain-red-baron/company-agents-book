# Jensen and Meckling on the Firm

Michael Jensen and William H. Meckling's 1976 paper [Theory of the firm: Managerial behavior, agency costs and ownership structure](https://EconPapers.repec.org/RePEc:eee:jfinec:v:3:y:1976:i:4:p:305-360) shifts attention from production to contracts and incentives. Their view is often summarized as the firm being a nexus of contracts.

## Sections

### Core Idea

In this lens, the company is not a seamless organism. It is a bundle of relationships among owners, managers, employees, creditors, and other parties. The hard problem inside the firm is not only coordination, but agency: how to make people act in line with the interests of those who bear the economic consequences.

### Details

Jensen and Meckling make the firm feel less like a single actor and more like a negotiated system of incentives. Owners want one thing, managers may want another, employees have their own interests, and capital providers care about downside protection and returns. The company therefore contains built-in tension.

That is why agency costs matter. Monitoring, reporting, incentive design, ownership structure, and governance mechanisms exist because interests do not automatically align inside a firm. This is an important counterweight to naive ideas about organizations behaving as if they had one coherent mind.

For your thesis, this is highly relevant. Even if a future company runs with one human and many agents, the problem of alignment does not disappear. It changes form. You still need to ask who defines goals, who monitors behavior, who bears consequences, and how the system avoids drifting away from the founder's intent.
