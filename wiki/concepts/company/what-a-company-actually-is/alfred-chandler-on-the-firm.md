# Alfred Chandler on the Firm

Alfred Chandler's work treats the company as a managerial enterprise whose power comes from administration, coordination, and organizational capability. The historical summary in [Remembering Alfred Chandler](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/remembering-alfred-chandler) captures his view that firms are more than contracts because they develop routines, knowledge, and managerial coordination internally.

## Sections

### Core Idea

Chandler's firm is the modern managerial enterprise. It becomes important when managers can coordinate activity and allocate resources more effectively than decentralized exchange alone. In this lens, the company is a designed administrative machine that creates capabilities through management, learning, and scale.

### Details

Chandler adds historical and managerial depth to the theory of the firm. He shows that large firms did not merely get bigger because they owned more capital. They became powerful because they developed administrative systems that could coordinate production, distribution, and strategy across growing scale.

This turns management into a productive force. The company is not just a legal shell with contracts inside it. It is an institution that learns how to allocate resources, structure work, and build enduring capability. That is why Chandler matters so much for a book about agent management: he helps frame management itself as a scalable technology.

If earlier firms became dominant through managerial innovation, then one of the live questions in your book is whether agent management becomes the next major managerial innovation.
