# Legal Personhood and the Company

From a legal perspective, a company is a person-like entity created by law. That legal status allows it to own property, enter contracts, sue and be sued, and continue beyond the lives of its founders.

## Sections

### Core Idea

The legal definition explains why a company can act as a durable container for economic activity. In [Britannica's corporation summary](https://www.britannica.com/summary/corporation), the corporation is presented as a legal form with limited liability, share transferability, juridical personality, and indefinite duration. In [Britannica's legal personhood entry](https://www.britannica.com/topic/legal-personhood), legal personhood is what allows a corporation to participate in the legal system as an entity.

### Details

The legal lens matters because it answers a very practical question: what exactly is the thing that owns the assets, signs contracts, employs people, raises capital, and survives founder turnover? The answer is not simply "the founder" or "the team." It is the legal entity.

That entity creates separation. The company can own property even though individual employees change. It can enter obligations even when management rotates. It can hold liability in a way that does not collapse directly back into the personal lives of every participant. For entrepreneurs, this is one of the first major transitions from hustle to companyhood: the moment the activity is no longer just you doing business, but a legally recognized container that can persist.

This lens is incomplete on its own, because it does not explain why firms exist economically or how they become capable. But it establishes the boundary condition that the other theories build on.

### Why It Matters

This lens does not tell you everything about what a company is, but it tells you why a company can hold assets, protect investors, and survive management turnover. It is the outer shell that makes the rest of the company possible.
