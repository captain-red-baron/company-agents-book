# Edith Penrose on the Firm

Edith Penrose moves the theory of the firm inside the company. In [The Theory of the Growth of the Firm](https://doi.org/10.1093/0198289774.003.0001), she treats the firm as a bundle of productive resources whose growth depends on how management deploys and recombines them.

## Sections

### Core Idea

Penrose's perspective matters because it makes the company more than a legal wrapper or contract bundle. The firm becomes a living accumulation of knowledge, managerial attention, and productive services that can be used in new ways. Growth is constrained not just by capital, but by the capacity to organize and absorb more activity.

### Details

Penrose is especially valuable because she helps explain why companies are not interchangeable shells. Two firms with similar products can have very different internal strengths because they have accumulated different managerial knowledge, routines, and ways of using resources.

Her idea of productive services is important here. Resources are not valuable in isolation. They become valuable through what management can do with them. That means growth is not only about adding more money or more headcount. It is also about whether the organization can actually absorb and redeploy resources intelligently.

This is a strong bridge to an AI-native company. If agents expand the productive services available to one founder or small leadership core, then the practical growth constraints of a company may shift from labor scarcity toward orchestration quality.
