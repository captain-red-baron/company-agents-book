# Processes, Routines, and Operating Systems

Structure alone does not make a company work. A company becomes operational through repeatable processes, routines, and information flows that let people act without renegotiating every step from scratch.

## Sections

### Core Idea

March and Simon's [Organizations](https://www.wiley-vch.de/en/areas-interest/finance-economics-law/business-management-13ba/organization-management-theory-13ba2/organizational-behavior-13ba21/organizations-978-0-631-18631-1) is useful here because it links organizations to participation rules, communication patterns, planning, and performance programs. Simon's [Administrative Behavior](https://mitpressbookstore.mit.edu/book/9780684835822) also reinforces that administration is built around decision processes, not just hierarchy charts. In practice, the company runs on operating logic, not on legal form.

### Details

Processes and routines are where the abstract organization becomes real. They define how leads are handled, how products are shipped, how bugs are fixed, how hiring works, how approvals happen, and how exceptions are escalated.

In early-stage companies, these systems are often embedded in the founder's head. Over time, that becomes a bottleneck. The company matures by turning implicit operating logic into shared repeatable routines. That is one reason SOPs, workflows, dashboards, and cadence systems matter so much.

This is one of the clearest bridges to the future company in your book. If agents become major contributors, then prompts, workflows, review loops, and orchestration rules become part of the company's operating system in a very literal sense.

### Why It Matters

This matters a lot for agent-native firms. If your future company is one human plus many agents, then the real company will live inside prompts, workflows, escalation rules, review loops, and system boundaries.
