# Structure, Roles, and Spans of Control

Companies are built through structure: functions, business units, reporting lines, and spans of control. Structure determines how work is divided, who coordinates whom, and how much complexity one manager can absorb.

## Sections

### Core Idea

Alfred Chandler's work on the managerial enterprise is central here because he shows that modern firms become powerful when administrative coordination scales. James March and Herbert Simon also matter because they connect structure to bounded rationality, communication, conflict, and participation. Structure is not just a chart. It is a way of controlling attention and coordination cost.

### Details

Structure is the visible architecture of the firm. It defines who reports to whom, how work is divided, where decisions sit, and how much complexity each leader or manager is expected to handle.

Many founders treat structure as bureaucratic overhead until growth forces the issue. But structure is what allows a company to divide labor without dissolving into confusion. It shapes communication paths, escalation routes, and the effective span of control of each leader.

This page matters directly to your book because the one-human-many-agents model is really a claim about structure. It suggests that agents could massively expand the effective span of control of a human operator. That would not eliminate structure. It would compress and redesign it.
