# Legal Shell, Ownership, and Boundaries

The first layer of a company is legal. The legal shell defines where the company begins and ends, who owns it, what assets sit inside it, what liabilities it carries, and which contracts it can sign.

## Sections

### Core Idea

The legal-entity view matters because it gives the company continuity and separation from its founders. [Britannica's corporation summary](https://www.britannica.com/summary/corporation) highlights limited liability, transferability of shares, juridical personality, and indefinite duration as core features of the corporation. Those features create a durable boundary around business activity.

### Details

If you strip the company down to its first architectural layer, you get boundaries. What is inside the entity? What belongs to the founder personally? Who owns the intellectual property? Who signs customer agreements? Who holds the cash? Who bears liability when something goes wrong?

These questions are not side issues. They determine whether the business can scale, raise money, enter long-term contracts, and survive changes in personnel. The legal shell also creates a definable unit that governance and management can act upon.

For a one-human-many-agents company, this layer still matters. The fact that execution becomes more software-driven does not eliminate the need for a durable legal boundary around assets, responsibility, and control.

### Why It Matters

Without a boundary, there is no stable company architecture. The legal shell is what lets the organization own resources, define control rights, and persist long enough to build processes and capabilities.
