# What an Agent Is — Anchor Page

The concept of an agent is one of the most stable ideas in computer science, and one of the oldest ideas in business and law. Across every domain, the core meaning is the same: an entity that acts on behalf of someone else to achieve goals in an environment. What changes across domains is the vocabulary, the emphasis, and the implementation — not the fundamental structure.

---

## The Definitional Consensus

Despite coming from different traditions, nearly all agent definitions converge on four properties:

1. **Situated**: The agent exists within an environment it can perceive
2. **Autonomous**: The agent acts without continuous human direction
3. **Goal-directed**: The agent's behavior is organized around achieving objectives
4. **Adaptive**: The agent adjusts its behavior based on feedback from the environment

Classical computer science researchers (Russell and Norvig, Wooldridge and Jennings, Franklin and Graesser, Maes) arrived at this consensus independently through the 1990s. Modern industry definitions from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, IBM, and AWS restate the same properties in LLM-native vocabulary — replacing "sensors" with "context windows" and "actuators" with "tool calls," but preserving the architecture.

The business and legal definition of a human agent predates all of these by centuries. An agent in law is a person authorized to act on behalf of a principal, bound by fiduciary duties, operating within a defined scope of authority. The structural parallel is exact.
