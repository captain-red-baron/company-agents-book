# The Human Agent

The oldest definition of "agent" is not from computer science. It is from law and business. For centuries, an agent has meant a person authorized to act on behalf of another — the principal. This definition predates software, predates AI, and predates computers entirely. It is the original template, and it maps onto AI agents with startling precision. Understanding what a human agent is, and why the concept exists, is the key to understanding what AI agents can become.

## Sections

### Core Idea

In business and law, an **agent** is a person who has the legal right to act on behalf of another person or business (the **principal**). The agent operates under delegated authority, within a defined scope, bound by fiduciary duties to act in the principal's best interests. This principal-agent relationship is the foundational structure of every employment contract, every management hierarchy, and every delegation of work. It is also, precisely, the structure of an AI agent deployment.

### Details

#### The Legal and Business Definition

An agent in the legal sense has three defining characteristics:

1. **Acts on behalf of a principal** — The agent does not act for themselves. They represent and execute for someone else.
2. **Operates under delegated authority** — The principal grants the agent a defined scope of what they can and cannot do. Authority may be express (explicitly stated), implied (reasonably inferred from the role), or apparent (third parties reasonably believe the agent has it).
3. **Bound by fiduciary duty** — The agent must act in the best interests of the principal, not their own. This includes duties of loyalty, care, obedience, and disclosure.

Types of agents in business:

- **General Agent** — authorized to act in all business matters within a specific area (e.g., a branch manager)
- **Special Agent** — appointed for a specific task or transaction (e.g., a real estate agent selling one property)
- **Employee-Agent** — a person hired to act within the scope of their employment
- **Independent Contractor** — performs tasks with more autonomy over how the work is done, but still acts on behalf of the principal

Every employee in every company is, in legal and economic terms, an agent of the firm. The entire structure of organizational management — job descriptions, reporting lines, performance reviews, compensation structures, authority matrices — exists to make the principal-agent relationship work at scale.

#### The Principal-Agent Problem

Economics has studied the challenges of agency for decades. The **principal-agent problem** (also called the agency problem) arises because:

1. **Information asymmetry** — The agent knows more about their own actions, effort, and local conditions than the principal can observe
2. **Goal divergence** — The agent's personal goals may not perfectly align with the principal's objectives
3. **Monitoring costs** — The principal cannot perfectly observe everything the agent does

The result: agents may optimize for what they are rewarded for (or what is easy) rather than what the principal actually wants. This is not malice — it is a structural feature of delegation under imperfect information.

Companies have developed extensive mechanisms to manage this:
- **Incentive alignment** — compensation structures, bonuses, equity
- **Monitoring** — management oversight, reporting, audits
- **Cultural norms** — shared values, team identity, social pressure
- **Authority boundaries** — clear scopes of decision-making
- **Escalation protocols** — defined triggers for when the agent should defer to the principal
- **Performance evaluation** — regular assessment against explicit criteria
- **Trust building** — progressive expansion of autonomy based on demonstrated competence

These mechanisms are not bugs in organizational design. They are the core technology of management.

#### Why This Definition Matters for AI Agents

The structural parallel between human agents and AI agents is exact:

| Element | Human Agent | AI Agent |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Principal** | Employer, client, manager | User, system administrator, organization |
| **Delegated authority** | Job description, authority matrix | System prompt, permission scopes, tool access |
| **Fiduciary duty** | Legal and ethical obligation to act in principal's interest | Constitutional AI, RLHF, safety guardrails |
| **Information asymmetry** | Agent knows more about local conditions and own effort | Agent "knows" more about its reasoning process and has access to more data |
| **Goal divergence** | Personal goals vs. organizational goals | Reward hacking, specification gaming, hallucination |
| **Monitoring** | Management oversight, 1:1s, reporting | Logging, evaluation, human-in-the-loop |
| **Trust building** | Progressive responsibility based on track record | Progressive autonomy based on demonstrated reliability |

The principal-agent problem is not an analogy for AI alignment. It is the *same problem* manifesting in a new substrate. The mechanisms that companies use to manage human agents — incentive design, authority boundaries, monitoring, escalation, trust progression — are directly applicable to AI agent deployment.

### Why It Matters

The human agent definition is the oldest, most battle-tested, and most practical framework for understanding agency. Companies have spent centuries refining the mechanisms that make delegation work under imperfect conditions. These mechanisms — incentive design, authority boundaries, monitoring, escalation, performance evaluation, progressive trust — are not theoretical constructs. They are the daily operating system of every functional organization.

The opportunity is that this entire body of management knowledge transfers to AI agents. You do not need to invent new management theory for AI agents. You need to adapt existing management practice. The principal-agent problem is the same. The delegation challenge is the same. The trust-building process is the same. What changes is the speed, scale, and replicability of the agent — and those differences require adjustments, not reinvention.
